## AI Learning Design Guidance

August 2024
*To be reviewed at the end of Q4.* 

 This document is for anyone designing content about artificial intelligence (AI) and designing practical activities that use AI. For guidance on using AI tools, please refer to our [Generative AI Principles](Generative%20AI%20Principles.md). ([Latest version here](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1pAyg1mcfM49k60OamReofKhLm4BhDN9CKcaNIwLqRcg/edit?usp=sharing)) 

  The purpose of this guidance is to ensure consistency in how we discuss AI across our products and to establish clear boundaries regarding what we will and will not encourage learners to do.

**The following is guidance for how we design AI content:** 

- Any references to AI tools must align with our Gen AI principles  ([Latest version here](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1pAyg1mcfM49k60OamReofKhLm4BhDN9CKcaNIwLqRcg/edit?usp=sharing)) and include appropriate warnings.

- We should demonstrate good practice by being transparent about where AI has been used and encourage others to follow our example.

- We should reinforce that generative AI tools do not reliably produce factually correct and coherent information. All AI content MUST be thoroughly reviewed.

- We should remind users that AI tools are generally not to be used by children under 13. Children over 13 should use AI-generated material responsibly, not share it without acknowledging it is AI-generated, and seek guidance from a responsible adult where concerns arise.

- Where appropriate our work on AI is research informed. (Quick Read coming soon) Start with these blogs and consult the research centre team where appropriate:
	- [Blog on ML education / Computational thinking 2.0](https://www.raspberrypi.org/blog/machine-learning-education-school-computational-thinking-2-0-research-seminar/)
	- [Blog on Anthropomorphism](https://www.raspberrypi.org/blog/ai-education-anthropomorphism/)
	- [Blog on SEAME framework](https://www.raspberrypi.org/blog/ai-education-resources-what-to-teach-seame-framework/)
  
- Before starting our design work, we review existing AI content to gain a broad understanding of the foundations current work in this area (linked below)

- We link to our [educator training course](https://www.raspberrypi.org/courses/ai-and-machine-learning) to support those teaching AI concepts. 

- Due to the widespread confusion surrounding AI concepts, it's crucial that we use precise vocabulary and share a common understanding of these terms. Refer to this [glossary](https://experience-ai.org/en/drive_resources/52.pdf) to help with this.  

- When we create resources, we often assume no prior knowledge on AI or ML. We should therefore always include a short, 1-2 sentence description of ML and AI, focusing on: data-driven models, large data-sets, pattern recognition and prediction.

- Avoid anthropomorphising AI in our content: 
	- Use system-oriented language ("detect," "analyse," "generate") instead of words that imply human thought or action ("recognise," "create," "decide"). 
	- Ensure all images reflect AI as a tool, not a sentient being. This clarity helps learners understand that AI is a human-designed tool with limitations, promoting critical thinking about its applications and potential biases.

- Use the [SEAME model](https://www.raspberrypi.org/blog/ai-education-resources-what-to-teach-seame-framework/) to frame the learning objectives and time allocation of learning activities. This ensures that Social and Ethical concerns are as important as the more technical aspects. 

- Clearly distinguish and highlight the difference between data-driven and the traditional rule-based approach to program development, [adopt the CT2.0 view of Matti Tedre](https://www.raspberrypi.org/blog/machine-learning-education-school-computational-thinking-2-0-research-seminar/) as a guide. This is essential to ensure that learners understand that ML is a predictive not deterministic approach.

- When selecting examples of ML to use, consider if the type of ML engine is appropriate to the learning objective and the longer term flow of  knowledge building and mental models to be formed ([see decision tree decision](https://docs.google.com/document/d/17xvNGCXuw1iWZFnS0vv64RXVijvCifdeyE06XY3SDmQ/edit)).

**When designing content or practical activities that use AI  for learners under 13**

- For learners under 13, we focus on teaching what AI is, AI literacy and its potential risks, rather than instructing on how to use it. We do not direct learners to access LLMs directly. For example, whilst we should create resources teaching young people to be critical thinkers in relation to AI technologies such as LLMs, we do not direct learners to access tools with age restrictions inappropriate for their age 

**When designing practical activities that use AI for learners 13- 18 and 18+**

- We only design practical activities that use AI to support clear learning objectives, which could include raising awareness but not solely for the sake of engagement.

- LLM system providers and some Government bodies stipulate age restrictions or provide age-related guidance on direct access to LLM products (e.g. through prompt engineering). Make sure you are aware of these restrictions and guidance for the jurisdictions that products are being designed for and that we abide by these requirements.
	- For instructor-led activities, before encouraging young learners, within the age-restriction, to use prompt engineering to access LLM applications, ensure that instructors have acknowledged the related risks and that instructors and students have completed education activities on risks and safe use.
	- For independent activities,  before encouraging young learners, within the age-restriction, to use prompt engineering to access LLM applications, ensure that there is a notice on the content to make parents and learners aware of the related risks and that students have completed education activities on risks and safe use.
	- When directing learners to LLMs that require parental/carer/educator consent, we must clearly communicate this requirement.

**When designing practical activities that use AI for Educators:**

- Ensure you are directing educators to the terms and conditions of the LLM application to make them aware of any restrictions in their country. 

- All content must advise educators of the pitfalls and risks as well as sharing any benefits.
